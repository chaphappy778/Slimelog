Part  One:  Base  Textures  
The  foundation  of  every  slime.  Base  textures  describe  the  overall  body  and  behavior  of  the  slime  —  how  
it
 
stretches,
 
holds,
 
drizzles,
 
or
 
pops.
 
Most
 
named
 
slime
 
styles
 
are
 
variations
 
layered
 
atop
 
one
 
of
 
these
 
bases.
  
Glue  Types  
Modern  slime  is  generally  composed  of  PVA  glue  and  boric  acid.  
White  Glue  A  general  category  for  all  opaque  white  glues.  •  Elmer's  School  Glue  •  Elmer's  Glue-All  •  Aleene's  Tacky  Glue  •  Amazon  School  Glue  •  Craft  Smart  White  Glue  •  Cra-Z-Art  All  Purpose  Glue   
Clear  Glue  A  general  category  for  all  transparent  glues.  •  Elmer's  Clear  Glue  •  Amazon  Clear  Glue  •  Craft  Smart  Clear  Glue  •  Cra-Z-Art  Clear  Glue   
Wood  Glue  Glue  primarily  intended  for  woodworking  projects.  •  Elmer's  Wood  Glue  Max  •  Titebond  Original  Wood  Glue   
K  Glue  A  broad  category  of  glues  from  Korea.  •  Amos  Craft  Glue  •  Amos  Liquid  Glue  •  JONG  IE  NARA  Craft  Glue  •  JONG  IE  NARA  Liquid  Glue

Fluffy   
Definition:  Fluffy  slime  gets  its  pillowy,  airy  body  from  shaving  foam  folded  into  a  white  glue  base.

 Note:  Fluffy  is  at  its  best  in  the  first  few  hours  after  mixing.  Shaving  foam  gradually  loses  its  structure,  so

 Variants  &  Related:  Cloud,  Cloud  Cream.  Similar  airy  vibes,  different  science:  those  rely  on  snow  powder

Floam   
Definition:  Floam  is  a  glue  base  packed  with  foam  beads  for  a  crunchy,  beady  texture.  How  densely

 Note:  Bead  density  changes  everything.  A  lightly  beaded  floam  and  a  fully  saturated  one  sound  and  feel

 Variants  &  Related:  Float,  Crunchy,  Bead  Bomb.  These  run  from  the  light  and  airy  Float  up  to  fully  loaded

Snow  Fizz  
Definition:  Extremely  thick  and  extremely  crunchy,  snow  fizz  is  made  with  plastic  snow,  an  instant  snow

 Variants  &  Related:  Crunchy,  Bingsu.  Similar  crunchy,  crumbly  profiles  reached  through  different

Beaded   
Definition:  Beaded  slime  is  defined  by  its  bingsu  or  fishbowl  beads,  which  deliver  maximum  crunch  and

 Note:  When  rating  a  beaded  slime,  pay  close  attention  to  bead  type  and  bead  size.  Each  bead  brings  its

Clay  
Definition:  A  soft  clay  base  that  yields  smooth,  soft,  thick  slime.  Clay  is  the  building  block  behind  butter

slay

 Note:  You  will  see  clay  more  often  as  an  ingredient  in  hybrid  textures  than  as  a  standalone  finished

Sand  
Definition:  Built  on  a  kinetic  sand  base,  sand  slimes  are  grainy,  moldable,  and  crumble  most  satisfyingly.  Note:  Sand  slimes  sit  right  on  the  border  between  slime  and  kinetic  sand,  and  some  collectors  draw  the

Sugar  Scrub

These
 
are
 
what
 
transform
 
a

Beads  
Foam  Beads  Lightweight  polystyrene  beads  (sometimes  called  'styrofoam  beads')  in  various  sizes.  Produce  a  soft  
crunch
 
and
 
are
 
the
 
defining
 
ingredient
 
of
 
floam
 
slimes.
 
Fishbowl  Beads  Small,  transparent,  hollow  plastic  beads  shaped  like  miniature  half-spheres  or  domes.  Suspended  in  clear  
slime,
 
they
 
produce
 
a
 
loud,
 
glassy
 
crunch.
 
Bingsu  Beads  Thin,  shaved-ice-like  translucent  strips.  Named  after  the  Korean  shaved  ice  dessert.  Produce  a  fine,  
delicate
 
crunch
 
distinct
 
from
 
fishbowl
 
beads.
 
Slushie  Beads  Soft,  jelly-like  translucent  beads  with  a  yielding  squish.  Often  used  to  evoke  icy  or  drink-themed  slimes.  
Micro  Beads  Very  small  beads  (sub-millimeter)  that  produce  a  fine,  dense,  sand-like  texture  in  slime.  Common  in  
micro
 
dough
 
variants.
 
Bead  Bombs  Not  a  bead  type  but  a  slime  style  —  a  slime  with  maximum  bead  loading,  often  combining  multiple  bead  
types
 
in
 
a
 
single
 
product.
 
The
 
'bomb'
 
refers
 
to
 
the
 
dense,
 
layered
 
concentration.
 
Charms  &  Toppings  
Charms  Small  plastic  or  resin  decorative  pieces  —  often  food-themed  (donuts,  fruit,  pastries)  or  
character-themed.
 
Charms
 
are
 
typically
 
placed
 
on
 
top
 
of
 
the

Sprinkles  or  FIMO  Tiny  shaped  polymer  clay  pieces,  usually  mimicking  baking  sprinkles,  fruit  slices,  or  confetti.  Mixed  in  or

top.

Structural  Add-ins  
Air  Dry  Clay  Soft,  lightweight  clay  (such  as  Daiso  or  Model  Magic)  mixed  into  a  glue-based  slime  to  produce  butter,  
slay,
 
and
 
clay
 
textures.
 
The
 
defining
 
structural
 
ingredient
 
of
 
butter
 
slimes.
 
Shaving  Foam  Pressurized  shaving  cream  added  to  white  glue  slime  to  produce  fluffy  slime.  Adds  volume  and  softness  
but
 
breaks
 
down
 
over
 
time.
 
Lotion  Used  to  soften  and  add  stretch,  especially  in  butter,  custard,  and  cream  cheese  textures.  Also  affects  
scent
 
profile.
 
Iron  Filings  Magnetic  metal  particles  suspended  in  slime  to  create  magnetic  slime.  
Sugar  Granules  Used  in  sugar  scrub  slime  for  a  gritty,  exfoliating  texture.  
Wax  Used  as  a  base  or  additive  in  wax,  wax-coated  clay  toppers,  and  wax-cracking  slimes.  
Activators  
Activators  cross-link  the  PVA  in  glue  to  form  slime.  Most  commercial  slimes  use  a  borate-based  activator.  
Borax  Solution  Borax  dissolved  in  warm  water.  Strong  activator  —  easy  to  over-activate,  producing  stiff,  rubbery  slime.  
Contact  Lens  Solution  Multipurpose  contact  solution  containing  boric  acid  and  sodium  borate.  The  most  common  at-home  
activator.
 
Brand
 
and
 
formula
 
matter
 
—
 
not
 
all
 
contact
 
solutions
 
activate
 
slime.
 
Liquid  Starch  A  milder  activator  that  produces  softer,  stretchier  slime.  Less  common  in  commercial  slime  but  popular  
in
 
DIY
 
recipes.
 
Boric  Acid  Pure  boric  acid  in  powder  or  solution  form.  Used  by  makers  for  precise  control  over  activation.

Part  Three:  Containers  &  Packaging  
The  container  is  part  of  the  product.  Slime  containers  affect  storage,  freshness,  presentation,  and

experience
 
—

Container  Types  
Plastic  Deli  Container  Round  or  square  plastic  container  with  a  tight-fitting  lid.  The  industry  workhorse  —  durable,  airtight,  and  
stackable.
 
Common
 
sizes:
 
4oz,
 
6oz,
 
8oz,
 
16oz,
 
32oz.
 
PET  Jar  Clear  plastic  jar,  often  with  a  screw-on  lid.  Showcases  the  slime  through  the  container  wall  and  is  
preferred
 
for
 
visual
 
products
 
like
 
clear,
 
jelly,
 
and
 
avalanche
 
slimes.
 
Glass  Jar  Higher-end  packaging  used  by  some  boutique  shops.  Heavier  and  breakable  but  offers  a  premium  
presentation.
 
Less
 
common
 
due
 
to
 
shipping
 
risk.
 
Squeeze  Tube  Soft  plastic  tube  with  a  flip-cap  or  screw  lid.  Used  for  runnier  textures  like  water  and  jiggly  slimes  that  
pour
 
rather
 
than
 
scoop.
 
Themed  Containers  Custom-shaped  containers  designed  around  a  slime  theme  —  e.g.,  bear-shaped,  fruit-shaped,  or  
character-shaped
 
vessels.
 
A
 
signature
 
element
 
of
 
premium
 
and
 
themed
 
drops.
 
Sizes  
Slime  is  typically  sold  by  weight  in  ounces.  Standard  sizes:  •  4oz:  Sample  or  mini  size.  Common  for  new  shop  trials  or  limited  drops.  •  6oz:  Most  common  standard  size  in  the  U.S.  slime  market.  •  8oz:  Larger  standard,  sometimes  called  'full  size'  depending  on  the  shop.  •  16oz:  Pint-size  —  a  value  or  premium  offering.  •  32oz:  Quart-size  —  bulk  or  hero  product,  often  used  for  restock  specials.  
Packaging  Add-ons  
Lid  Stickers  Branded  or  themed  stickers  applied  to  the  container  lid.  A  primary  branding  surface  and  often  a

Slime  is  a  living  material.  Even  a  perfectly  mixed  batch  will  change  over  time,  and  how  it  ages  depends

Storage  Conditions  
Sealed  Storage:  Keep  slime  in  its  original  airtight  container  with  the  lid  fully  closed  when  not  in  use.  PET

and

 Temperature:  Room  temperature,  roughly  65  to  75  degrees  Fahrenheit,  is  ideal.  Cold  storage  can  stiffen  
slime
 
and

Warm

 Light:  Direct  sunlight  will  fade  pigments,  accelerate  scent  breakdown,  and  degrade  clear  bases.  Store  
slimes

Lifespan  Expectations  
A  well-stored  slime  typically  maintains  its  core  texture  for  one  to  three  months  and  remains  playable  for  
six
 
months

Specific

 ●  Floam,  Snow  Fizz,  Beaded:  longest  lifespan;  bead-based  textures  hold  up  well  over  time.  ●  Butter,  Slay,  Clay-based:  several  months  with  light  maintenance.  ●  Cloud,  Cloud  Cream,  Fluffy:  shorter  peak  window;  airy  textures  break  down  faster.  ●  Wax  and  Wax  Cracking:  relatively  new  category;  long-term  lifespan  is  still  being  established.

Add

to
 
avoid

 Adding  Lotion:  For  slime  that  has  become  stiff  or  rubbery,  unscented  lotion  can  soften  and  restore

Adding
 
lotion

 Adding  Glue:  For  slime  that  has  dried  significantly  or  become  crumbly,  a  small  addition  of  fresh  glue  can

 Adding  Foam  or  Snow:  Fluffy  and  cloud  slimes  that  have  deflated  may  be  partially  refreshed  by  adding

 Scent  Boosters:  Faded  scent  can  be  refreshed  with  a  small  amount  of  fragrance  oil  or  a  packaged  scent

Part  Eleven:  Sound  &  ASMR  Vocabulary  
Slime  sounds  are  a  primary  driver  of  ASMR  appeal  and  are  often  as  important  to  collectors  as  visual  or

Different

Foundational  Sounds  
Drizzle:  The  soft,  ribboning  sound  of  slime  falling  in  a  thin  stream.  Sizzle:  Fine  crackling  or  hissing  as  small  air  pockets  release—most  associated  with  icee  and  cloud  cream.  Pop:  Audible  bubble  burst  when  air  pockets  in  thick  slime  collapse.   
Crunch  Family  
Crunch:  A  general  term  for  the  sound  of  bead-  or  snow-loaded  slime  being  squished  or  stretched.  Wet  Crunch:  Crunch  with  audible  moisture,  often  from  beaded  slimes  with  heavy  clear-glue  saturation.  Dry  Crunch:  Crisp,  papery  crunch  typical  of  snow  fizz  and  bingsu-loaded  slimes.  Glassy  Crunch:  Sharp,  high-pitched  crunch  from  fishbowl  bead  slimes.   
Soft  Sounds  
Squish:  Soft,  yielding  compression  sound  from  butter,  slay,  and  cloud  cream  textures.  Squelch:  Wetter  version  of  squish,  often  from  cloud  creams  and  high-moisture  slimes.  Slosh:  Liquid-wave  sound  of  water  and  jiggly  slimes  when  moved  in  a  container.   
Stretch  and  Movement  Sounds  
Click  /  Clicky:  Repetitive  small  popping  sounds  during  stretching—a  classic  of  thick  &  glossy.  Cascade:  The  continuous  rolling  pop  as  thick  slime  is  poked,  with  air  bubbles  collapsing  in  sequence.  Slap:  Solid,  dense  impact  sound  when  a  thick  slime  hits  a  surface.  Crack:  Sharp  audible  split.  The  defining  sound  of  wax-cracking  slimes.   
Mixed  and  Hybrid  Sounds  
Layered  Sounds:  Slimes  with  multiple  texture  types  (e.g.,  a  beaded  butter)  produce  composite  sound  
profiles
 
that
 
combine
 
elements
 
of
 
each
 
base.
 Inflation  Sound:  The  soft  hiss  and  stretch  of  inflating  slime,  followed  by  the  burst.
